Finding 2: The Latent Space SabotageThe Flaw in the Paper: The authors claim they found "adversarial attention" because they scrambled the attention weights, but the final output prediction $\hat{y}$ didn't change by more than a small threshold $\epsilon$. This is a massive logical leap. Just because the final classification boundary wasn't crossed does not mean the model's internal reasoning remained the same.The Experiment: You will measure the shift in the model's internal latent space, rather than just looking at the final softmax output.The Math: Let $h_{orig}$ be the final pooled representation vector (e.g., the CLS token output) before the classification head using the original attention. Let $h_{adv}$ be the final representation vector using their adversarial attention. Calculate the cosine similarity between the two vectors:$$\text{sim}(h_{orig}, h_{adv}) = \frac{h_{orig} \cdot h_{adv}}{\|h_{orig}\|_2 \|h_{adv}\|_2}$$Where:$\text{sim}(h_{orig}, h_{adv})$ measures the angle and directional alignment between the two representations.$h_{orig} \cdot h_{adv}$ is the dot product of the vectors.$\|h_{orig}\|_2$ and $\|h_{adv}\|_2$ are the L2 norms (magnitudes) of the respective vectors.The Result to Present: Show that while the final predicted class is the same, $\text{sim}(h_{orig}, h_{adv})$ is near zero. This proves empirically that the adversarial attention did not find an "equally plausible explanation". Instead, it completely destroyed the model's internal representation, forcing it into an entirely different region of the latent space that just coincidentally mapped to the same side of the final linear decision boundary. This destroys their argument that counterfactual attention weights are valid alternative explanations.
